# **Bibliography from Critical Theory Seminar**

### Simon Malpas and Paul Wake, *The Routledge Companion to Critical and Cultural Theory*
**Summary:**  
A broad overview of major concepts, traditions, and debates in critical and cultural theory.

**How I can use it:**  
Best used as general background rather than as a core thesis source.

### Jen McWeeny, “Liberating Anger, Embodying Knowledge: A Comparative Study of María Lugones and Zen Master Hakuin”
**Summary:**  
A comparative philosophical study connecting anger, embodiment, and knowledge.

**How I can use it:**  
Potentially useful for embodied knowledge, but not a direct fit with my main typographic framework.

### Ji Xihou and Xia Kejun, “Dialectics of Blandness: The Contemporaneity of Su Shi’s Aesthetics”
**Summary:**  
A discussion of Su Shi’s aesthetics and the concept of blandness as a complex artistic value.

**How I can use it:**  
Interesting for aesthetics, but it seems too distant from my main research focus unless I build a very specific argument about form.

### Jill Beaulieu and Mary Roberts, *Orientalism’s Interlocutors: Painting, Architecture, Photography*
**Summary:**  
A study of visual culture, architecture, photography, and representation through the lens of Orientalism.

**How I can use it:**  
Relevant to representation more broadly, but not as directly useful to my thesis as the postcolonial readings above.

### Simone de Beauvoir, *Feminist Writings*
**Summary:**  
A collection of foundational feminist philosophical and political writings.

**How I can use it:**  
Important historically, but not among the most direct sources for my thesis.

### Sue Thornham, *Feminist Film Theory: A Reader*
**Summary:**  
A collection of texts on feminist approaches to cinema, spectatorship, and representation.

**How I can use it:**  
Helpful for visual theory in general, but not central to my typography and corridor focus.

### Slavoj Žižek, *The Sublime Object of Ideology*
**Summary:**  
A major work on ideology, subjectivity, and psychoanalysis.

**How I can use it:**  
Theoretically rich, but likely too abstract for the core of my thesis.

### Jacques Lacan, *Écrits: A Selection*
**Summary:**  
Foundational psychoanalytic writings on language, subjectivity, desire, and structure.

**How I can use it:**  
Important theoretically, but not the most efficient source for my project.

### Lee Edelman, *No Future: Queer Theory and the Death Drive*
**Summary:**  
A critique of politics organized around futurity, reproduction, and social normativity.

**How I can use it:**  
Conceptually strong, but not directly tied to my main thesis focus.

### **Dunne, Anthony, and Fiona Raby. _Speculative Everything: Design, Fiction, and Social Dreaming_.**  

**Summary**: A well-known design theory book that proposes speculative design as a way to imagine alternative futures and social possibilities.  

**Why it is here:** It is relevant to design research and critical making, but it is less directly tied to your core themes of archive, multilingual reading, ethical visibility, and corridor life.

### **Loveless, Natalie. _How to Make Art at the End of the World: A Manifesto for Research-Creation_.**  

**Summary**: A manifesto-like book on research-creation, interdisciplinarity, and art-making as a critical academic practice.  

**Why it is here:** It is highly useful for your broader methodology, but it works better as a general research-creation framework than as a direct source for one of your five thesis themes.
