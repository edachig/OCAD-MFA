# **3. Typographic mechanics and multilingual reading**

Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar

## **Gloria Anzaldúa, The Gloria Anzaldúa Reader**

**Summary**: A collection of Anzaldúa’s writings on border identity, language, hybridity, embodiment, spirituality, and lived contradiction.

**Why it fits:** One of the strongest sources for your thesis because it connects multilingual life, border thinking, and identity with language and form.

## **Sara Ahmed, Queer Phenomenology: Orientations, Objects, Others**

**Summary**: Ahmed explores how bodies are oriented in space and how social norms shape what feels familiar, reachable, and intelligible.

**Why it fits**: Very useful for thinking about reading as orientation, and about how typographic systems guide some readers more comfortably than others.

## **Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Phenomenology of Perception**

**Summary**: A philosophical study of perception as embodied, lived, and relational rather than abstract or purely mental.

**Why it fits**: Helps you frame typographic reading as an embodied act shaped by vision, habit, movement, and environment.

## **Nadja Eisenberg-Guyot and Kitty Rotolo, “A Trans Way of Seeing”**

**Summary**: A reflection on visuality, recognition, and the politics of how bodies and identities are seen.

**Why it fits**: Useful for questioning assumed norms of legibility and recognition in visual systems.

## **David Bell, Cyberculture Theorists: Manuel Castells and Donna Haraway**

**Summary**: Introduces key digital culture thinkers and debates around networks, bodies, technology, and contemporary mediated life.

**Why it fits**: More secondary, but can help if you want to connect multilingual reading to digital environments and new media alphabets.

## **Jones, “Let’s get physical: Supporting arts-based research through haptic learning”**

**Summary**: Argues for tactile, embodied, and sensory approaches to research and learning.

**Why it is here**: It supports your idea that reading and typographic engagement are embodied processes, not only abstract visual acts.

## **Sirgado de Sousa, “Visions of sound and colour: Generating visuals and sound from a source photographic image”**

**Summary**: Explores translation across media by generating visual and sonic outcomes from photographic input.

**Why it is here:** It fits your interest in moving from environmental references and local imagery into typographic form, especially when letterforms emerge through transformation and interpretation.

## **Barker, “Experiments with time: the technical image in video art, new media and the digital humanities”**

**Summary**: Looks at time-based media and the technical image in video and digital practice.

**Why it is here:** It is useful if your typographic work includes animation, variable states, or digital specimens that unfold over time rather than existing only as static print.

## **Anzures and Marques, “The LED lamp metaphor: Knowledge and the creative process in new media art”**

**Summary**: Discusses knowledge production through creative process in new media art.
**Why it is here:** It helps justify your process-led experimentation and shows how form can emerge through making, iteration, and technological exploration.

## **Gibson, James J. “The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception.”**

**Summary**: A major work on perception that argues vision is shaped by environment, movement, and embodied relation to the world.

**Why it is here:** It is one of the strongest texts for this category because it helps frame reading as embodied and environmental. It supports your view that typographic mechanics are not neutral, but shaped by perceptual habits, context, and lived conditions.
