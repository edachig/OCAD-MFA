# **3. Typographic mechanics and multilingual reading**

Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar

## **Gloria Anzaldúa, The Gloria Anzaldúa Reader**

**Summary**: A collection of Anzaldúa’s writings on border identity, language, hybridity, embodiment, spirituality, and lived contradiction.

**Why it fits:** One of the strongest sources for your thesis because it connects multilingual life, border thinking, and identity with language and form.

## **Sara Ahmed, Queer Phenomenology: Orientations, Objects, Others**

**Summary**: Ahmed explores how bodies are oriented in space and how social norms shape what feels familiar, reachable, and intelligible.

**Why it fits**: Very useful for thinking about reading as orientation, and about how typographic systems guide some readers more comfortably than others.

## **Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Phenomenology of Perception**

**Summary**: A philosophical study of perception as embodied, lived, and relational rather than abstract or purely mental.

**Why it fits**: Helps you frame typographic reading as an embodied act shaped by vision, habit, movement, and environment.

## **Nadja Eisenberg-Guyot and Kitty Rotolo, “A Trans Way of Seeing”**

**Summary**: A reflection on visuality, recognition, and the politics of how bodies and identities are seen.

**Why it fits**: Useful for questioning assumed norms of legibility and recognition in visual systems.

## **David Bell, Cyberculture Theorists: Manuel Castells and Donna Haraway**

**Summary**: Introduces key digital culture thinkers and debates around networks, bodies, technology, and contemporary mediated life.

**Why it fits**: More secondary, but can help if you want to connect multilingual reading to digital environments and new media alphabets.

## **Jost Hochuli, Detail in Typography**

**Summary**:
Jost Hochuli’s Detail in Typography is useful for understanding how small typographic decisions affect reading. The book focuses on details such as letter spacing, word spacing, line spacing, line length, type size, margins, paragraph structure, hierarchy, and the overall rhythm of text. Hochuli shows that readability is not only created by the typeface itself, but also by how the typeface is arranged, spaced, and used in a page or text system.

**Why it is here:** Typographic mechanics and multilingual reading because it gives you the technical language to analyze how typography performs in real reading conditions. In your thesis, you are not only designing letterforms, but also testing how they work across languages, scales, formats, and contexts. Hochuli helps you explain that readability depends on many mechanics: spacing, rhythm, density, proportion, and layout.

For your project, this is important because multilingual typography cannot be judged only by whether a letter looks beautiful. It must also be tested through paragraphs, accents, special characters, bilingual text, signage, posters, specimens, and digital environments. Hochuli gives you a foundation for discussing those details carefully.

## **## Cyrus Highsmith, Inside Paragraphs: Typographic Fundamentals**

**Summary**:
Cyrus Highsmith’s Inside Paragraphs explains how reading happens inside the structure of a paragraph. The book focuses on the relationship between letters, words, lines, spacing, rhythm, density, and white space. Instead of looking only at individual letterforms, Highsmith shows how typography functions as a reading system, where small decisions affect the texture and clarity of a paragraph.

**Why it is here:**
The thesis is not only about designing alphabets, but also about testing how those alphabets work in real reading conditions. Highsmith helps you study what happens when letters become words, words become lines, and lines become paragraphs. This is especially useful for multilingual reading because accents, special characters, word length, spacing, rhythm, and paragraph texture can shift depending on the language being used.
