# **4. Ethical visibility**

Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar

## **Donna J. Haraway, Staying with the Trouble: Making Kin in the Chthulucene**

**Summary**: Haraway argues for situated, relational, accountable ways of living and thinking rather than distant, universal, detached models.

**Why it fits:** Very strong for your methodology because it supports a relational, co-created, and ethically grounded design practice.


## **Julia Emberley, Defamiliarizing the Aboriginal: Cultural Practices and Decolonization in Canada**

**Summary**: Examines Indigenous cultural representation in Canada and the politics of decolonization, perception, and cultural practice.

**Why it fits**: Strong Canadian-context reading for thinking through ethical representation and the limits of visibility.

## **Reuben Rose-Redwood et al., “Decolonizing the Map: Recentering Indigenous Mappings”**

**Summary**: Critiques colonial mapping traditions and argues for Indigenous spatial knowledge and alternative practices of place-making.

**Why it fits**: Useful for your project because it helps you think about place, naming, representation, and whose spatial knowledge becomes visible.

## **Danika Medak-Saltzman, “Empire’s Haunted Logics: Comparative Colonialisms and the Challenges of Incorporating Indigeneity”**

**Summary**: Examines how colonial structures continue to shape discourse and representation even within critical scholarship.

**Why it fits:** Helps you stay alert to the ways representation can reproduce colonial habits even when it appears progressive.

## **Tyna Fritschy, “Queer Indiscipline, Decolonial Revolt: The Production of Undisciplined Knowledges in the Neoliberal Age”**

**Summary**: Argues for forms of knowledge that resist disciplinary control, normativity, and neoliberal categorization.

**Why it fits**: Useful for framing your project as a practice that resists flattening, extraction, and over-classification.

## **Vaage, “What ethics for bioart?”**

**Summary**: Examines ethical questions in experimental art practices, especially around responsibility and representation.

**Why it is here:** Even though it is about bioart, it gives you a useful ethics framework for thinking about how your project presents cultural material and lived experience responsibly.	

## **Buolamwini, “Compassion through computation: Fighting algorithmic bias”**

**Summary**: Shows how systems of recognition can misread or exclude people.

**Why it is here:** It directly supports your concern with visibility by showing that making people visible in systems is not automatically ethical if the system itself is biased

## **Luccioni, “AI is dangerous, but not for the reasons you think”**

**Summary**: Offers a critical view of AI and its broader risks beyond popular panic narratives.

**Why it is here:** It is relevant if you use digital tools in your practice and want to discuss issues of simplification, bias, and extraction in technical systems.

## **Watfern et al., “A sad tree: visualising ecological emotions through bodies in place”**

**Summary**: Explores how emotion, visualization, embodiment, and place can be brought together in research.

**Why it is here**: It is useful for your ethical framing because it models a careful and affective way of representing experience without flattening it into neutral data

## Puig de la Bellacasa, María. Matters of Care: Speculative Ethics in More than Human Worlds.

**Summary**: A book on ethics, care, relationality, and responsibility in more-than-human worlds.

**Why it is here**: It is very useful for your thesis because it helps frame representation as an ethical practice of care, not simply a matter of making people visible.

## Barad, Karen. “Transmaterialities: Trans/ Matter/ Realities and Queer Political Imaginings.”

**Summary**: A theoretical text on fluidity, matter, identity, and the instability of fixed categories.

**Why it is here:** It fits this category because ethical visibility in your project depends on resisting reductive categories and allowing for complexity, transition, and non-fixity.

## **Barad, Karen, et al. “On-Touching-The Inhuman That Therefore I Am.”**

**Summary**: A reflection on touch, relationality, and the entanglement of bodies, matter, and perception.

**Why it is here:** It is useful because it emphasizes relation and entanglement, which can help you think about visibility as something negotiated and relational rather than detached or extractive.

## **Simpson, Leanne Betasamosake. “Land as Pedagogy: Nishnaabeg Intelligence and Rebellious Transformation.”**

**Summary**: A text that positions land as a living teacher and a basis for resistant forms of knowledge.

**Why it is here:** It belongs here because it reminds you that ethical representation must be grounded in relation, accountability, and place rather than imposed from outside.

## **Derrida, Jacques. “Hostipitality.”**

**Summary**: A philosophical text on the tension between hospitality and hostility, especially in relation to welcoming the other.

**Why it is here:** It is useful for your thesis because it helps you think about the risks and contradictions involved in making others visible, included, or represented.
