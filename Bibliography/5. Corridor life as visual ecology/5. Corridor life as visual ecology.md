# **5.Corridor life as visual ecology**

Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar

## **Ihab Hassan, “Extraterritorial: Exile, Diaspora, and the Ground under Your Feet”**

**Summary**: Reflects on exile, diasporic life, displacement, and the unstable relationship between identity and place.

**Why it fits:** Strong for your corridor focus because it links belonging, displacement, and lived geography.

## **Virinder Kalra, John Hutnyk, and Raminder Kaur, “Hybridity and Openness (or, Whose Side Are You On?)”**

**Summary**: A critical discussion of hybridity and its political uses, especially in relation to migration, identity, and cultural difference.

**Why it fits**: Useful for understanding corridor life as culturally mixed, negotiated, and shaped by power

## **Klaus Stierstorfer and Florian Kläger, eds., Diasporic Constructions of Home and Belonging**

**Summary**: A collection on how diasporic communities construct ideas of home, identity, and belonging across locations.

**Why it fits**: Good for framing the London-Sarnia corridor as a lived geography of attachment, movement, and settlement.

## **Zakiya Luna, “Location Matters: The 2017 Women’s Marches as Intersectional Imaginary”**

**Summary**: Examines how location shapes collective identity, politics, and experience through an intersectional lens.

**Why it fits**: Helps reinforce your point that geography is active and formative, not just background.

## **Giuliana Bruno, “Projections: The Architectural Imaginary in Art”
**
**Summary**: Explores architecture, surface, projection, and spatial imagination in visual culture and art.

**Why it fits**: Useful if you want to discuss storefronts, facades, interiors, and built space as part of your visual reference system.

## **Diaspora & Hybridity**

**Summary**: A text that explores diasporic identity, hybridity, migration, and the politics of cultural mixing.

**Why it fits:** Best as broader support for understanding the corridor as a site of movement and layered belonging.

## **Camargo-Borges, “Creativity and worldmaking: Research as worldmaking”**

**Summary**: Presents research as an active process of shaping worlds, relations, and meanings rather than simply observing them.

**Why it is here:** It helps you frame the London-Sarnia corridor as a lived and produced environment that actively informs your typographic research.

## Watfern et al., “A sad tree: visualising ecological emotions through bodies in place”

**Summary**: Connects bodies, place, feeling, and visual research.

**Why it is here:** It is one of the strongest readings here for understanding place as something lived, sensed, and emotionally inhabited, which fits your visual ecology idea.

## **Obomsawin, Kanehsatake: 270 Years of Resistance**

**Summary**: A documentary rooted in land, struggle, community, and memory.

**Why it is here:** It helps you think about environment and place as active historical forces rather than neutral background.

## **Tuck, Eve, and Marcia McKenzie. “Decolonizing Perspectives on Place.”**

**Summary**: A decolonial discussion of place, land, and spatial knowledge that critiques dominant settler understandings.

**Why it is here:** It is strongly connected to your corridor framework because it helps you think of the London-Sarnia corridor as an active, political, and lived environment rather than a neutral backdrop.

## **Gibson, James J. “The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception.”**

**Summary**: A foundational study of perception as ecological, embodied, and relational.

**Why it is here:** It supports your idea of visual ecology by showing that seeing is shaped by movement, environment, surfaces, and lived spatial conditions.

## **Simpson, Leanne Betasamosake. “Land as Pedagogy: Nishnaabeg Intelligence and Rebellious Transformation.”**

**Summary**: A text about land-based knowledge, Indigenous intelligence, and place as a site of teaching and transformation.

**Why it is here**: It is valuable for grounding corridor life in more than movement and infrastructure. It helps frame land itself as a knowledge-bearing presence.

## **Wall Kimmerer, Robin. Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge, and the Teachings of Plants.**

**Summary**: A book that connects ecology, reciprocity, Indigenous thought, and practices of attention.

**Why it is here**: It supports your visual ecology theme by helping you think relationally about environment, care, and the living world that surrounds and shapes human movement.

## **Little Bear, Leroy, and Gregory Cajete. “Developing Resonance with the Sky Spirits of the Cosmos: Flux and Native Astronomy.”**

**Summary**: A text that engages Indigenous cosmology, flux, and ways of understanding the world through relation and resonance.

**Why it is here**: It is useful because it expands your sense of ecology beyond the built corridor and into broader Indigenous understandings of movement, relation, and environment.
