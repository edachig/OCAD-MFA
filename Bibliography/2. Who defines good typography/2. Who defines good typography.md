	Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar

## **Richard A. Lynch, “Foucault’s Theory of Power”**

**Summary**: Explains Foucault’s view that power is not only top-down repression, but also something that produces norms, knowledge, and accepted truths.

**Why it fits**: Very helpful for arguing that typographic standards such as readability, professionalism, and correctness are socially produced, not universal.

## **Gurminder K. Bhambra, “Decolonizing Critical Theory?”**

**Summary**: Bhambra critiques the Eurocentric foundations of critical theory and argues for more inclusive and historically accountable frameworks.

**Why it fits:** Useful for questioning whose aesthetic and linguistic standards have been treated as the default in typography and design.

## **Judith Butler, Gender Trouble: Feminism and the Subversion of Identity**

**Summary**: Butler argues that identity is not fixed or natural, but produced through repeated social norms and performances.

**Why it fits:** Helpful for thinking about typographic norms as repeated conventions that become naturalized over time.

**Vicki Kirby, Judith Butler: Live Theory**

**Summary**: An accessible engagement with Butler’s ideas, especially around identity, discourse, and the instability of norms.

**Why it fits:** A supporting text if you want a more readable theoretical bridge into normativity and constructed standards.

## Homi K. Bhabha, The Location of Culture

**Summary**: A major postcolonial text on hybridity, ambivalence, mimicry, and the instability of cultural authority.

**Why it fits**: Useful for challenging fixed standards of typographic quality and for questioning purity in language and design conventions.

## Sofia Vranou, “Performing the Subcultural Freak: Leigh Bowery’s Peculiar Narcissism and the Disruption of Normativity”

**Summary**: Examines Bowery’s performance practice as a deliberate disruption of normality, taste, and acceptable social form.

**Why it fits**: Good for thinking about how what is dismissed as excessive, improper, or illegible can expose the instability of dominant norms.

## Colonial Discourse and Post-colonial Theory: A Reader edited by Laura Chrisman and Patrick Williams

**Summary**: A collection of foundational postcolonial texts on empire, representation, power, and cultural difference.

**Why it fits**: Best used as background support for questioning inherited Western standards in design and representation.

## **Gerrit Noordzij, The Stroke: Theory of Writing**


**Summary:**
Gerrit Noordzij’s The Stroke: Theory of Writing explains how letterforms are built from the movement of the stroke. Instead of treating typography as a fixed visual style, Noordzij studies writing as a system of movement, contrast, direction, pressure, white space, and construction. The book is important because it connects handwriting, calligraphy, and typography, showing that printed and digital type still carry traces of written gestures and tools. Hyphen Press describes the book as a theory of writing that applies to both Western and non-Western writing systems, beginning with the white space around letters and then explaining how strokes form written shapes.

**Why it is here:**
This book belongs in Theme 2: Who defines “good typography” because it helps you question where typographic standards come from. Noordzij shows that “good” typography is not only about beauty, correctness, or professional rules. It is also shaped by inherited writing systems, tools, hand movement, and the way designers are trained to see letters. For your thesis, this helps challenge the idea that typographic quality is universal. Instead, you can argue that what is considered “good” typography often comes from specific traditions of writing, education, and institutional design history.

## **Jost Hochuli, Detail in Typography**

**Summary**: Jost Hochuli’s Detail in Typography focuses on the small typographic decisions that shape how a text is read: letter spacing, word spacing, line spacing, line length, type size, margins, hierarchy, and page rhythm. The book treats typography as a careful practice of adjustment, where quality often depends on details that are almost invisible to the reader but strongly affect clarity and comfort.

**Why it is here:** It represents a clear example of professional typographic standards. Hochuli explains what is usually considered “good” typography within a European modernist/book typography tradition: balanced spacing, controlled rhythm, legibility, proportion, and visual order.
