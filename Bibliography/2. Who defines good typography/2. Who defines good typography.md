# **2.	Who defines good typography**

	Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar

## **Richard A. Lynch, “Foucault’s Theory of Power”**

**Summary**: Explains Foucault’s view that power is not only top-down repression, but also something that produces norms, knowledge, and accepted truths.

**Why it fits**: Very helpful for arguing that typographic standards such as readability, professionalism, and correctness are socially produced, not universal.

## **Gurminder K. Bhambra, “Decolonizing Critical Theory?”**

**Summary**: Bhambra critiques the Eurocentric foundations of critical theory and argues for more inclusive and historically accountable frameworks.

**Why it fits:** Useful for questioning whose aesthetic and linguistic standards have been treated as the default in typography and design.

## **Judith Butler, Gender Trouble: Feminism and the Subversion of Identity**

**Summary**: Butler argues that identity is not fixed or natural, but produced through repeated social norms and performances.

**Why it fits:** Helpful for thinking about typographic norms as repeated conventions that become naturalized over time.

**Vicki Kirby, Judith Butler: Live Theory**

**Summary**: An accessible engagement with Butler’s ideas, especially around identity, discourse, and the instability of norms.

**Why it fits:** A supporting text if you want a more readable theoretical bridge into normativity and constructed standards.

## Homi K. Bhabha, The Location of Culture

**Summary**: A major postcolonial text on hybridity, ambivalence, mimicry, and the instability of cultural authority.

**Why it fits**: Useful for challenging fixed standards of typographic quality and for questioning purity in language and design conventions.

## Sofia Vranou, “Performing the Subcultural Freak: Leigh Bowery’s Peculiar Narcissism and the Disruption of Normativity”

**Summary**: Examines Bowery’s performance practice as a deliberate disruption of normality, taste, and acceptable social form.

**Why it fits**: Good for thinking about how what is dismissed as excessive, improper, or illegible can expose the instability of dominant norms.

## Colonial Discourse and Post-colonial Theory: A Reader edited by Laura Chrisman and Patrick Williams

**Summary**: A collection of foundational postcolonial texts on empire, representation, power, and cultural difference.

**Why it fits**: Best used as background support for questioning inherited Western standards in design and representation.

	Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar
	
## **Chimero, The Shape of Design (Chapters 5 and 6)**

**Summary**: Reflects on design judgment, process, form, and the values behind what designers consider effective or meaningful.

**Why it is here:** It helps you question how design quality is judged and supports your argument that “good typography” is contextual, constructed, and not universal.

## **Buolamwini, “Compassion through computation: Fighting algorithmic bias”**

**Summary**: Explains how technical systems can reproduce bias while appearing neutral or objective.

**Why it is here**: It supports your critique of typographic standards by showing that supposedly neutral systems often reflect dominant assumptions and exclusions.

	Bibliography from: Direct Studio 1

## **Barad, Karen. “Transmaterialities: Trans/ Matter/ Realities and Queer Political Imaginings.”**

**Summary**: A theoretical text that challenges fixed categories of matter, identity, and reality, proposing a more fluid understanding of form and being.

**Why it is here:** It helps question rigid standards and fixed definitions. In your thesis, it can support the argument that typographic norms are not natural or universal, but historically and politically constructed.

## **Tuck, Eve, and Marcia McKenzie. “Decolonizing Perspectives on Place.**

**Summary**: A text that critiques dominant understandings of land and place while proposing decolonial ways of thinking spatially and relationally.

**Why it is here:** It is relevant because standards of design and readability are shaped by dominant worldviews. This reading helps question whose perspectives become normalized in visual and spatial systems.

## **Barad, Karen, et al. “On-Touching-The Inhuman That Therefore I Am.”**

**Summary**: A theoretical reflection on touch, relationality, and the boundaries between human and nonhuman experience.

**Why it is here:** It is here because it unsettles stable categories and fixed boundaries, which can help you challenge the idea that typographic correctness or quality comes from neutral, universal rules.

## **Simpson, Leanne Betasamosake. “Land as Pedagogy: Nishnaabeg Intelligence and Rebellious Transformation.”**

**Summary**: A foundational text arguing that land is not just setting, but a source of knowledge, teaching, and Indigenous intellectual practice.

**Why it is here**: It helps challenge dominant institutional frameworks of knowledge. In your thesis, it can support a critique of who gets to define proper systems of reading, design, and cultural value.
