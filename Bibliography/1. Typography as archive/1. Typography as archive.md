# **1. Typography as archive**

	Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar

## **[## **- Thomas King, The Truth About Stories**](https://ocadu.alma.exlibrisgroup.com/leganto/public/01OCUL_OCAD/citation/2976588540007481?auth=SAML)**

**Summary**: A reflection on how stories shape identity, history, power, and collective understanding. King shows that storytelling is never neutral and always has consequences.

**Why it fits:** Very useful for thinking of typography as a carrier of memory, lived experience, and cultural record, not just formal communication.


## **- Walter Benjamin, The Work of Art in the Age of Its Technological Reproducibility, and Other Writings on Media**

**Summary**: Benjamin examines how reproduction changes the meaning, circulation, and authority of art and media.

**Why it fits**: Helps you frame alphabets, prints, and typographic artifacts as reproducible cultural forms that circulate across media and preserve or transform meaning.


## **- Raphaël Ouellet, “From Forms to Form: Contemporary Art and the Practice of Bureaucratic Everyday Life”**

**Summary**: Looks at how ordinary administrative and bureaucratic forms can become meaningful aesthetic and cultural material in art practice.

**Why it fits**: Strong for your interest in everyday documents, signage, and ordinary graphic traces as part of a typographic archive.

## **- Audre Lorde, Sister Outsider**

**Summary:** A collection of essays and speeches on difference, identity, voice, anger, power, and feminist politics.

**Why it fits:** Useful when you want to think of testimony, voice, and lived experience as forms of cultural record.

	Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar

## **- Beck, “Doing it” in the kitchen: Rhetorical field methods, arts/practice-based research, and queer archives**

**Summary**: Examines how arts-based and practice-based research can work through everyday spaces, lived experience, and queer archival methods.

**Why it is here**: It directly supports your idea that typography can act as archive by showing how research and making can preserve cultural traces outside official institutions.

## **Fiordelmondo et al., “A multilevel dynamic model for documenting, reactivating and preserving interactive multimedia art”**

**Summary**: Focuses on methods for documenting, preserving, and reactivating multimedia artworks over time.

**Why it is here**: It is useful for thinking about how your typographic work, especially digital and variable outputs, can be documented as part of an archive rather than treated as a single final object.

## **Obomsawin, Kanehsatake: 270 Years of Resistance**

**Summary**: A documentary film that records resistance, memory, and historical struggle from a community-centered perspective.

**Why it is here**: It is a strong model for understanding media as testimony and cultural record, which is close to your idea of typography as evidence and archive.

## **Robertson, Making Memories: Alanis Obomsawin**

**Summary**: A film about Alanis Obomsawin’s media practice and the role of memory, storytelling, and cultural preservation.

**Why it is here**: It helps position creative practice as a form of memory work, which supports your understanding of typographic making as archival work.

Bibliography from: Direct Studio 1


## **Kim, Mihyun Maria. The Measure of Han. Master’s Thesis, OCAD University**

**Summary**: A graduate thesis that appears to examine han as a cultural, emotional, and historical condition through artistic or design research.

**Why it is here:** It fits this theme because it suggests how form, feeling, and cultural memory can be carried through creative practice. It can help support your idea that typography can hold testimony, affect, and cultural record.

## **Wall Kimmerer, Robin. Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge, and the Teachings of Plants.**

**Summary**: A book that brings together Indigenous knowledge, ecology, relationality, and ways of learning from the natural world.

**Why it is here:** It is useful here because it treats knowledge as living, relational, and carried through story and observation rather than only through official archives. That can help you frame typography as a form of cultural preservation and record.
