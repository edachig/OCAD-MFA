	Bibliography from: Critical Theory Seminar

## **[## **- Thomas King, The Truth About Stories**](https://ocadu.alma.exlibrisgroup.com/leganto/public/01OCUL_OCAD/citation/2976588540007481?auth=SAML)** *Pending*

**Summary**: A reflection on how stories shape identity, history, power, and collective understanding. King shows that storytelling is never neutral and always has consequences.

**Why it fits:** Very useful for thinking of typography as a carrier of memory, lived experience, and cultural record, not just formal communication.


## **- Walter Benjamin, The Work of Art in the Age of Its Technological Reproducibility, and Other Writings on Media**

**Summary**: Benjamin examines how reproduction changes the meaning, circulation, and authority of art and media.

**Why it fits**: Helps you frame alphabets, prints, and typographic artifacts as reproducible cultural forms that circulate across media and preserve or transform meaning.


## **- Raphaël Ouellet, “From Forms to Form: Contemporary Art and the Practice of Bureaucratic Everyday Life”**

**Summary**: Looks at how ordinary administrative and bureaucratic forms can become meaningful aesthetic and cultural material in art practice.

**Why it fits**: Strong for your interest in everyday documents, signage, and ordinary graphic traces as part of a typographic archive.

## **- Audre Lorde, Sister Outsider** *Pending*

**Summary:** A collection of essays and speeches on difference, identity, voice, anger, power, and feminist politics.

**Why it fits:** Useful when you want to think of testimony, voice, and lived experience as forms of cultural record.

# **Strikethrough: Typographic Messages of Protest**

Summary: This book studies protest graphics through typography, posters, signs, publications, and activist ephemera. It looks at how letterforms become political tools, organizing examples across themes such as resistance, voting, strikes, teaching, and love. It includes protest material from movements such as nineteenth-century antislavery broadsides, Paris 1968, the Black Panther newspaper, and AIDS crisis awareness posters

Why it is here: This is directly useful for your thesis because it shows typography as public voice, dissent, memory, and collective action. It supports your idea that typography is not only a neutral design system, but also a cultural and political archive. For your project, it can help frame co-designed alphabets as messages of visibility, resistance, and community presence.

# **Safari tipográfico: La tipografía como recurso didáctico para la comunicación en la docencia online y aprendizaje del Diseño **

**Summary:** This article presents Safari tipográfico, a design education project where students used photography to explore typography in their own cities and environments. Instead of learning typography only through the screen, students documented signs, storefronts, and local letterforms, creating an archive of found typography while learning typographic classification, observation, and creative interpretation.

**Why it is here:** This is important for your thesis because it connects typography, place, learning, and visual documentation. It gives you a useful model for treating the everyday environment as a typographic archive. For your London–Sarnia corridor research, this source can support methods such as walking, photographing, observing local signage, and collecting community-based typographic references.

# **Protest Cultures : A Companion**

**Summary:** This edited volume expands the study of protest beyond politics and sociology by examining protest as culture, media, performance, communication, and collective expression. It combines broader theoretical essays with case studies of different protest movements and cultural practices.

**Why it is here:** This source is useful because it helps you situate typography within wider protest culture, not only as graphic design but as part of social movements, public expression, and collective memory. It can support your thesis when discussing how visual forms, signs, posters, and public messages become tools for representation, identity, and political visibility.
