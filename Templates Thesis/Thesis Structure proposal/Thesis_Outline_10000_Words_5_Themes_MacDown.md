# Typographic Archives of Latino Life: New Media Alphabets in Southwestern Ontario

## 10,000-Word Thesis Structure Based on the Five Themes

This outline restructures the thesis for a shorter **10,000-word** written component. The structure keeps the project academic, but also leaves space for creative practice, visual documentation, reflective writing, and the five research themes.

The recommended structure is **five chapters**:

1. **Introduction**
2. **Site, Community, and Visual Ecology**
3. **Theoretical Framework and Literature Review**
4. **Methodology and Creative Practice**
5. **Analysis, Reflection, and Conclusion**

The five themes are not treated as five separate chapters. Instead, they become the conceptual framework that appears across the thesis.

## Five Research Themes

| Theme | Focus |
|---|---|
| **Theme 1: Typography as archive** | Typography as memory, evidence, testimony, and cultural record. |
| **Theme 2: Who defines “good typography”?** | Typographic standards, authority, legibility, design education, and cultural assumptions. |
| **Theme 3: Typographic mechanics, readability, and multilingual conditions** | Spacing, hierarchy, rhythm, paragraph structure, accents, diacritics, extended Latin, and multilingual reading. |
| **Theme 4: Ethical visibility and representation** | Consent, co-authorship, reciprocity, care, and avoiding extraction. |
| **Theme 5: Corridor life, labour, movement, and visual ecology** | Latino life along the London-Sarnia corridor, migration, labour, signage, movement, and everyday visual culture. |

## Overall Word Count Distribution

| Chapter | Title | Main Purpose | Words |
|---|---|---|---:|
| **1** | Introduction | Present the project, research question, context, and five themes. | **1,300** |
| **2** | Site, Community, and Visual Ecology | Explain the London-Sarnia corridor and Latino visual culture. | **1,500** |
| **3** | Theoretical Framework and Literature Review | Build the academic argument through the five themes. | **2,800** |
| **4** | Methodology and Creative Practice | Explain research methods, co-design, type design, printmaking, and the making process. | **2,500** |
| **5** | Analysis, Reflection, and Conclusion | Analyze what the project reveals and conclude the contribution. | **1,900** |
|  | **Total** |  | **10,000** |

## Thesis Flow

The strongest flow for a 10,000-word thesis is:

**Introduction → Site → Theory → Method and Making → Analysis and Reflection**

This structure works because it moves from the larger project context into the specific place, then into theory, then into methods and creative practice, and finally into analysis and reflection.

## 3.1 Typography as Archive

**Suggested length: 550 words**

This section discusses typography as memory, evidence, testimony, and cultural record. The goal is to show that typography can do more than present language. It can also hold traces of lived experience, place, migration, labour, and identity.

### Key Ideas

- Typography as archive.
- Letterforms as cultural evidence.
- Storytelling and memory.
- Printed matter as a record of community life.
- Type specimens and posters as forms of documentation.

### Possible Argument

In this thesis, typography is treated not only as a tool for communication, but as a form of archive: a visual system capable of carrying memory, testimony, and traces of lived experience.

### References to Prioritize

| Reference | How to Use It |
|---|---|

| Thomas King | Use for storytelling, identity, history, power, and the consequences of representation. |
| Robin Wall Kimmerer | Use for relational knowledge, care, place, and forms of knowing beyond institutional systems. |

## 3.2 Who Defines “Good Typography”?

**Suggested length: 650 words**

This section questions typographic authority. It asks who decides what counts as good, legible, refined, professional, or correct typography.

### Key Ideas

- Typographic standards.
- Legibility and familiarity.
- Design education and authority.
- European typographic traditions.
- Craft versus cultural neutrality.
- Community-based standards of value.

### Possible Argument

This thesis does not reject typographic craft. Instead, it questions whether dominant standards of quality, refinement, and legibility are culturally neutral.

### Useful Contrast

| Typographic Craft References | Critical or Decolonial References |
|---|---|
| Jost Hochuli | Arturo Escobar |
| Gerrit Noordzij | Abdulla et al. |
| Sofie Beier | Tuck and McKenzie |
| Cyrus Highsmith | Design justice and community-based design approaches |

### Possible Sentence

Hochuli and Noordzij help define the internal craft of typography, including spacing, stroke, proportion, and detail. However, decolonial design theory allows this thesis to question who has historically had the authority to define these standards as universal.

## 3.3 Typographic Mechanics, Readability, and Multilingual Conditions

**Suggested length: 550 words**

This section focuses on the technical and cultural conditions of reading. It should explain that readability is not only optical or mechanical. It is also shaped by language, familiarity, education, cultural memory, and context.

### Key Ideas

- Spacing.
- Rhythm.
- Hierarchy.
- Paragraph structure.
- Scale.
- Accents and diacritics.
- Extended Latin characters.
- Multilingual reading.
- Familiarity and legibility.

### Possible Argument

Readability is technical, but it is also cultural. A typeface may be optically clear while still failing to support multilingual or diasporic reading conditions.

# What to Cut or Reduce for a 10,000-Word Thesis

| Cut or Reduce | Reason |
|---|---|
| Long historical background | Keep only what directly supports your argument. |
| Too many theorists per theme | Use two to four strong references per theme. |
| Separate long creative practice chapter | Combine creative practice with methodology. |
| Long descriptions of every artwork | Focus on the most important outputs and decisions. |
| Full technical type design explanation | Keep technical details connected to research questions. |
| Long conclusion | Make the conclusion reflective, precise, and contribution-focused. |
